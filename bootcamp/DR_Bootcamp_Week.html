The Power of the First 30 Seconds

Day 1Positive Introduction
Morning Huddle 30 minutes

Why This Matters

Outbound telesales is an interruption. The customer didn't ask for this call. You have 30 seconds to earn their willingness to stay on the line. The opening isn't just a formality -it's where trust is built or lost.

Catalyst Behaviors in Play: Engage Actively Own It -Warmth + competence from the first word.

What "Effectively Demonstrated" Looks Like

  • Genuine warmth -the customer hears a smile, not a script
  • Natural pace -not rushed, not robotic
  • Personal connection -find the small window between getting their name and launching into the pitch ("How's your day going?")
  • Pre-call prep (existing customers) -using the customer's name early shows you reviewed their account before dialing
  • Recording disclosure delivered -before any discovery questions, naturally woven in

The Compass Navigator Opening

"Hello! My name is [First Last], and I'm calling from Xfinity. May I speak with [Customer Name]?"
[Confirm identity, ask how their day is going]
"Before we continue, I need to inform you that the call is being recorded and monitored for the highest quality standards. For more information about Comcast privacy practices, please visit Xfinity.com/privacy."

Common Pitfalls

What DND sounds like
  • Robotic, memorized delivery
  • Flat tone -no warmth
  • Jumping straight from "Nice to meet you" into the pitch
  • Recording disclosure skipped or delivered after discovery
  • No personalization at all
What ED sounds like
  • Sounds like a real person, not reading
  • Pauses to let the customer respond
  • "I see you've been with us for 3 years -that's awesome!"
  • Recording disclosure feels natural, not rushed
  • Customer feels welcomed, not processed
Midday Follow-Up Throughout the day

Floor Walk Focus

  • Listen for the first 30 seconds of live calls -is the greeting warm or robotic?
  • Check: is the recording disclosure being delivered before discovery?
  • Spot-check: are agents using the customer's name on existing customer calls?

Quick Wins to Coach

  • If an agent sounds flat → have them stand up for their next call
  • If the disclosure sounds rushed → practice saying it at half speed, then speed up naturally
  • If they skip the personal moment → challenge them to ask one non-scripted question in the opening
Afternoon Focus 15 minutes

Debrief & Practice

  • Round-table: "What's one thing you changed about your opening today? What happened?"
  • Live practice: Pair up. One person is the customer, one is the agent. Do the first 30 seconds. Switch.
  • Challenge for tomorrow: Record yourself doing your opening before your first call tomorrow. Listen back. Would you stay on the line?

Discovering with Intent

Day 2Qualitative vs. Quantitative
Morning Huddle 30 minutes

Why This Matters

Discovery is the agent asking questions -not the customer asking you. It's the foundation of the entire pitch. A generic pitch happens because of generic discovery. A tailored pitch that closes happens because you uncovered something real.

Catalyst Behavior: Discover Needs -Ask proactive, relevant questions that reveal something new.

The 4 Pillars of Discovery

Users

Who lives in the home? Adults, kids, elderly? Remote workers?

"Who all is in the household using WiFi or devices?"

Usage

How do they use their services day to day? Streaming, gaming, WFH?

"Walk me through a typical evening -what's everyone doing on their devices?"

Devices

What are they connecting? Phones, tablets, smart home, gaming consoles?

"How many devices would you say are connected at any given time?"

Cost

What are they paying now? What matters financially?

"What are you currently paying for your Internet and phone service combined?"

Qualitative vs. Quantitative

Quantitative (Closed)

"Do you have WiFi?" → "Yes."
"Do you stream?" → "Yes."
"Do you have a phone?" → "Yes."

Result: Three questions, zero insight. You know nothing you didn't already know. The pitch that follows will be generic.

Qualitative (Open-Ended)

"Tell me about your household -who's home during the day and what are they usually doing online?"

Result: One question uncovers 3 pillars -Users, Usage, Devices. Now you can pitch with specificity: "Since your daughter is doing virtual school and your husband works from home..."

Key Rule: One great open-ended question that yields a full picture of the household is worth more than five closed questions that return one-word answers. Quality over quantity.
Midday Follow-Up Throughout the day

Floor Walk Focus

  • Listen for open-ended vs. closed questions -are agents asking "tell me about" or "do you have"?
  • Check: are agents covering all 4 pillars before pitching?
  • Listen for tie-backs being noted -are they writing down what the customer says?

Coaching Moment

  • If an agent jumps to pitch after one question → "What else could you have asked before presenting?"
  • If discovery sounds like a survey → "React to what they say. If they mention kids, ask about the kids."
Afternoon Focus 15 minutes

Debrief & Practice

  • Share-out: "What's the most interesting thing you learned about a customer today through discovery?"
  • Exercise: Write down 3 open-ended questions you want to try tomorrow. Share with a partner.
  • Challenge: Tomorrow, try to get a customer to tell you a story about their household in discovery. Not answers -a story.

Linking & Being the Ambassador

Day 3From Discovery to Recommendation
Morning Huddle 30 minutes

Why This Matters

Discovery without a tailored pitch is wasted effort. Being an Ambassador means you don't just list features -you connect them to what the customer told you. The customer should hear their own words reflected back in your recommendation.

Catalyst Behavior: Be an Ambassador -Speak confidently and proudly about Xfinity. Tie needs to value, not just features.

The Logic Bridge

Every recommendation needs a "because". The Consult phase follows this formula:

"Based on what you told me, I think I have the perfect plan for you..."

"I heard you say [what they told you in discovery], so I included [product] because [specific benefit for their household]."

Feature Lists vs. Recommendations

Feature List (DND)

"So our 1 Gig plan comes with unlimited data, a gateway, WiFi hotspot access, and Advanced Security. It's $60 a month with auto-pay."

Generic. Could be said to any customer. No connection to what was discovered.

Tailored Recommendation (ED)

"You mentioned your husband works from home and your kids are streaming constantly -so I'm recommending the 1 Gig plan because it gives your whole household the speed to do everything at once without anyone lagging. And since you said you're paying $80 right now, this actually brings you down to $60 with the same reliability."

Personal. References their household. Explains why it fits. Addresses cost.

Compliance Reminder: When pitching Internet or Mobile, you MUST reference broadband labels: "You can also see this information at Xfinity.com/labels." Weave it naturally into the pitch -don't bolt it on at the end.

Product Confidence

  • Know your products. If you hesitate or look things up mid-pitch, the customer loses confidence.
  • Pitch the right product. A household of heavy streamers needs Gig -not 300 Mbps. Match the product to the need.
  • Speak positively. "Our WiFi just works -99.9% reliability" beats "It's better than what you have."
  • Include the XM line. "You're also getting a phone line included for 12 months -that's $480 in savings on top of everything else."
Midday Follow-Up Throughout the day

Floor Walk Focus

  • Listen for the "because" -are agents connecting features to discovered needs?
  • Check: are agents using tie-back language ("You mentioned...", "I heard you say...")?
  • Listen for broadband labels -is it being referenced during the pitch?
  • Spot-check: is the right product being pitched for the household?
Afternoon Focus 15 minutes

Debrief & Practice

  • Exercise: Give each agent a fake customer profile (family of 4, heavy gamers, budget-conscious). Build a pitch in 60 seconds using tie-backs. Present to the group.
  • Discussion: "What product did you pitch today that you felt really confident about? What made you confident?"
  • Challenge: Tomorrow, start your pitch with "Based on what you told me..." on every call. See how it changes the customer's response.

Objections & Overcoming Them

Day 4The 4-Step Framework
Morning Huddle 30 minutes

Why This Matters

An objection is not a rejection -it's the customer telling you they need more information. The agents who close the most aren't the ones who never hear "no" -they're the ones who know what to do when they hear it.

Catalyst Behaviors in Play: Engage Actively Own It Be an Ambassador -Listen, take responsibility, and restate value.

The Compass Navigator 4-Step Objection Framework

  1. Understand the Objection
    Don't assume you know why they're hesitating. If the objection is vague ("I'll think about it"), probe:
    "I completely understand. Just so I can help -what specifically are you unsure about?"
  2. Empathize & Overcome
    Show you heard them before pivoting. Open with empathy, then bridge to the solution:
    "I completely understand that, however..."
  3. Restate Key Tie-Backs
    Anchor back to what they told you in discovery. This is why discovery matters:
    "I heard you say [X], so I wanted to make sure you had [benefit] -that's exactly why I recommended this plan."
  4. Add Additional Value
    Introduce one more benefit they haven't heard yet:
    "And on top of that, this plan also includes [new benefit] which would help with [their situation]."

Common Objections & Responses

"I need to think about it"

Understand: "Absolutely -what part are you thinking over?"

If price: Reframe value per day. "$60/mo is $2/day for your whole household."

"Too expensive"

Empathize: "I completely understand budget is important."

Restate: "You mentioned you're paying $80 now -this actually saves you $20/month."

"I'm under contract"

Understand: "When does your contract end?" → explore timing.

Value: "Even with an ETF, the savings over 12 months often exceed the fee."

"Not interested"

Probe: "Is it the product itself, or is this just not a good time?"

If timing: Respect it, but plant the seed with one benefit they'll remember.

Critical Rule: Never speak negatively about competitors. "Our service is 99.9% reliable" is good. "AT&T is unreliable" is a red flag (rf5).
Midday Follow-Up Throughout the day

Floor Walk Focus

  • Listen for objections -is the agent probing or immediately rebutting?
  • Check: after overcoming, does the agent re-close or just move on?
  • Listen for empathy -does "I understand" sound genuine or reflexive?

Coaching Moment

  • If an agent gives up after one "no" → "What could you have asked to understand the real concern?"
  • If the rebuttal sounds scripted → "Tie it back to something they told you earlier."
Afternoon Focus 15 minutes

Debrief & Role Play

  • Role play: Pair up. Customer throws an objection. Agent walks through all 4 steps. Switch roles.
  • Group discussion: "What's the hardest objection you heard today? How did you handle it?"
  • Challenge: Tomorrow, when you hear "I'll think about it," your first response must be a question, not a statement.

This Week's Behaviors in Review

Day 1 -Positive Introduction: Warmth from word one. Smile before you dial. Use their name. Deliver the disclosure naturally.

Day 2 -Discovery: Open-ended questions across Users, Usage, Devices, Cost. One great question beats five closed ones. Discovery is the agent asking, not the customer.

Day 3 -Linking & Ambassador: Every recommendation needs a "because." Use their words. Lead with benefits, not features. Broadband labels woven in.

Day 4 -Objections: Understand → Empathize → Restate → Add Value. Always probe before rebutting. Always re-close after overcoming.
